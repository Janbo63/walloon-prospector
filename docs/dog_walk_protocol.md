# Dog Walk Prospecting Protocol 🐕🚶‍♂️🧭

Turn your daily morning dog walks into systematic geological exploration. By standardizing how you search, record coordinates, and gather samples, your field data becomes the "Ground Truth" that trains your RAG models and feeds your shop's provenance engine.

---

## 🎒 1. Field Gear Checklist

Before leaving the house, make sure you have these essentials in your pockets or backpack:
*   **Smartphone**: With camera, GPS/map app (for coordinate pins), and a notebook app.
*   **Prospecting Bags**: Small ziplock bags or cloth bags numbered with a permanent marker (e.g., `#001`, `#002`).
*   **Permanent Marker**: To number bags or label rocks directly.
*   **Geologist's Loupe / Magnifier**: 10x magnification for close-up inspections.
*   **Small Spray Bottle**: Filled with water. Wetting a stone reveals its hidden colors and structures instantly.
*   **Safety / Boundaries**: Keep in mind property borders and Polish forestry laws (Lasy Państwowe guidelines on mineral collection).

---

## 🔎 2. What to Look For (High-Probability Spots)

Don't just look randomly; focus on natural erosions and historical indicators:
*   **Uprooted Trees**: When storm winds pull up tree roots, they dig up soil and rocks from deep underground. This is a goldmine for fresh, unweathered specimens.
*   **Stream & River Beds**: Running water naturally erodes rock, collects heavy minerals, and polishes quartz/gneiss. Look in dry gravel bars and bends in streams.
*   **Forest Path Tracks**: Erosion on sloped hiking or logging trails often exposes veins of quartz or clusters of feldspar.
*   **Quartz Outcrops**: Quartz is often associated with other minerals (chalcedony, amethyst, agate, and metals). Follow white quartz veins running through rock walls.
*   **Walloons Signs**: Historical carved symbols on rocks (crosses, hands, moons, triangles) or old, overgrown stone heaps (man-made adits or mining dumps).

---

## 📸 3. The 5-Step In-Field Collection Process

When you spot a stone that stands out, follow this workflow:

1.  **Wet and Inspect**: Spray water on the stone. Check it with your 10x loupe. If it looks interesting, proceed.
2.  **In-Situ Photo**: Take a clear picture of the stone *exactly where it lies* before picking it up. This preserves the geological context (matrix, surrounding soil, vegetation).
3.  **Log Coordinates**:
    *   Open your map/GPS app.
    *   Drop a pin at your current location.
    *   Copy the Decimal Degree (DD) coordinates (e.g., `50.912345, 15.387654`).
4.  **Bag and Label**: Place the specimen in a numbered bag (e.g., `#001`). Note the number down.
5.  **Quick Mobile Note**: Dictate or write a quick note on your phone:
    > *Bag #001: Quartz outcrop, milky white, found near old pine tree. Coordinates: 50.912345, 15.387654.*

---

## 🪨 4. Post-Walk Processing

When you return to the farm:
1.  **Cleaning**: Wash the specimen in warm water with a stiff brush to remove dirt. Let it dry.
2.  **Visual Log**: Take a well-lit photo of the clean specimen against a neutral background with a coin or ruler for scale.
3.  **Field Note Entry**: Create an entry in [field_notes/](file:///f:/Rocks/field_notes/README.md) using the coordinates and photos.
4.  **Sorting**:
    *   **Tumble Pile**: Hard stones (Quartz, Jasper, Agate - Mohs hardness 6.5+) go into the tumbling preparation container.
    *   **Cabinet Specimen**: High-quality crystals, large matrix pieces, or delicate minerals go straight to the shop specimen prep area.
    *   **Discard/Garden**: Return minor finds to the garden.
